## SECTION 02 | What Are Coaching Services?

**SECTION LABEL:** Understanding the Discipline

### What Are Coaching Services?

Coaching services consulting is a strategic advisory discipline that helps organizations design, implement, and optimize professional coaching programs to develop leaders, elevate team performance, and embed coaching as a core leadership capability. Unlike ad hoc coaching engagements where individual leaders hire personal coaches, enterprise coaching solutions take a systemic approach, aligning coaching investments to business strategy, matching coaches to organizational needs, measuring outcomes against defined KPIs, and building the internal infrastructure that sustains coaching impact at scale. **Executive coaching** develops C-suite and senior leaders through one-on-one engagements with experienced coaches who help them sharpen strategic thinking, navigate complex transitions, and amplify their organizational impact. **Leadership coaching** extends coaching access to mid-level managers, high-potential talent, and emerging leaders, building the pipeline of capable leaders the organization needs for succession and growth. **Team coaching** works with intact teams to improve collaboration, decision-making, accountability, and collective performance against shared objectives. **Coaching culture consulting** transforms coaching from a program into a pervasive organizational capability, training managers to coach their direct reports, embedding coaching conversations into daily operations, and creating the systems and mindsets that make coaching how work gets done. Together, these four dimensions of organizational coaching services create a comprehensive coaching ecosystem where individual development, team effectiveness, and organizational culture reinforce each other, producing leadership development coaching that scales and sustains.

Organizations worldwide recognize that coaching is one of the most effective leadership development interventions available, yet most struggle to move beyond fragmented, inconsistent coaching engagements that fail to produce enterprise-wide impact. *Icon: Gift box with question mark*
Most organizations treat coaching as a reward or remediation tool, offered to a handful of senior executives or assigned to struggling performers. There is no strategic framework connecting coaching investments to business priorities, succession planning, or talent pipeline development. Coaching happens in isolation without organizational alignment.
**Impact tag:** Only 25% of organizations have a formal coaching strategy tied to business goals

*Icon: Broken shield with certification badge*

The result is inconsistent coaching quality where outcomes depend entirely on individual coach capability rather than program design.
**Impact tag:** 40% of coaching engagements end without measurable outcomes due to poor coach-client matching

**CHALLENGE 3: Coaching Doesn't Reach the Middle**
*Icon: Organizational pyramid with gap in middle*
Executive coaching is reserved for the C-suite. Frontline training handles entry-level staff. But the critical middle layer, the directors, senior managers, and high-potential leaders who drive execution, receives little to no coaching support. This is precisely the population where coaching produces the highest organizational ROI.
**Impact tag:** Mid-level leaders are 3.5x more likely to leave without development support

*Icon: Empty dashboard screen*
When the CHRO asks "What did our coaching investment deliver?", most organizations cannot answer with data. Without structured outcome measurement, coaching remains an act of faith, vulnerable to budget cuts and impossible to optimize. Engagement satisfaction surveys are not ROI evidence. A coaching ROI consulting approach changes this entirely.
**Impact tag:** Only 13% of organizations measure coaching ROI beyond participant satisfaction

**CHALLENGE 5: Managers Can't Coach Their Teams**
*Icon: Speech bubble with broken connection*
Organizations invest in external coaches but neglect the most scalable coaching channel, their own managers. Research shows that managers who coach effectively drive 130% higher business performance, yet most managers lack the coaching skills, tools, and accountability structures to coach their direct reports consistently.
**Impact tag:** 69% of managers have never received coaching skills training

**CHALLENGE 6: Coaching Culture Exists Only on Paper**
*Icon: Document with disconnected nodes*
Many organizations declare they want a "coaching culture" but have no roadmap to build one. Without systemic changes to leadership behaviors, performance conversations, feedback mechanisms, and recognition systems, coaching culture remains an aspiration rather than an operating reality.

## SECTION 20 | FAQ

### Frequently Asked Questions About Coaching Services

**Q1: What is the difference between executive coaching, leadership coaching, and team coaching?**
Executive coaching is a one-on-one engagement between a senior leader (typically C-suite or VP level) and an experienced coach, focused on strategic leadership challenges such as executive presence, stakeholder influence, organizational transformation, and board effectiveness. Leadership coaching extends coaching access to mid-level managers, directors, and high-potential leaders, focusing on leadership pipeline development, new manager effectiveness, succession readiness, and the competencies needed to move from functional expertise to organizational leadership. Team coaching works with intact groups rather than individuals, helping leadership teams, cross-functional project teams, or newly formed groups improve their collective decision-making, mutual accountability, trust, and performance against shared objectives. The key distinction is the unit of development: executive coaching develops individual senior leaders, leadership coaching develops the broader leadership pipeline, and team coaching develops collective team capability. Most enterprise coaching programs include elements of all three, because organizational performance depends on strong individual leaders who can also work effectively together as teams.

**Q2: How do you measure the ROI of coaching programs?**
We use a multi-level measurement framework adapted from established evaluation methodologies. At Level 1, we measure participant satisfaction and coaching quality through structured feedback surveys. At Level 2, we assess learning and insight development, what participants gained in self-awareness, leadership understanding, and strategic thinking. At Level 3, we measure behavioral change, the degree to which coached leaders are demonstrating new behaviors on the job, assessed through self-report, manager observation, and 360-degree follow-up. At Level 4, we track business outcomes, linking coaching to measurable organizational metrics such as employee retention, team engagement scores, productivity improvements, promotion readiness acceleration, or revenue growth in coached leaders' areas of responsibility. At Level 5, we calculate financial ROI, converting business outcomes to monetary value, subtracting the fully loaded program cost, and expressing the net benefit as a percentage return on investment. Research from ICF and PricewaterhouseCoopers consistently shows a median coaching ROI of 7:1, meaning organizations receive seven dollars in value for every dollar invested in coaching. Edstellar's measurement framework ensures your organization can document and report this return with confidence.

**Q3: What qualifications should coaches have, and how does Edstellar vet its coaches?**
Professional coaches should hold credentials from recognized bodies, with the International Coaching Federation (ICF) being the global gold standard. ICF offers three credential levels: Associate Certified Coach (ACC) requiring 100+ coaching hours, Professional Certified Coach (PCC) requiring 500+ coaching hours, and Master Certified Coach (MCC) requiring 2,500+ coaching hours. Edstellar requires all coaches in our network to hold a minimum of ICF PCC or equivalent credential, with executive coaching engagements staffed by coaches with MCC-level credentials and documented C-suite coaching experience. Beyond credentialing, we vet coaches for industry expertise, cultural fit, coaching methodology alignment, and demonstrated outcomes with similar client populations. Every coach undergoes a multi-step evaluation: credential verification, reference checks with previous clients and sponsors, sample coaching session observation, and alignment interview with Edstellar's coaching quality team. We also maintain ongoing quality assurance through coachee feedback analysis, sponsor satisfaction tracking, and annual performance reviews for all coaches in our network. This rigorous vetting process is what distinguishes a coaching services firm from a marketplace platform.

**Q4: How long does a typical coaching engagement last, and how is it structured?**
Individual coaching engagements typically run 6 to 12 months, with sessions held bi-weekly (the most common cadence) or weekly for intensive engagements. A standard engagement structure includes: a kickoff phase (weeks 1-2) with stakeholder alignment, 360-degree assessment, and coaching agreement; a development phase (months 1-6) with regular coaching sessions focused on the agreed development objectives; and a sustainment phase (months 6-12) with reduced session frequency focused on embedding behavioral change, measuring outcomes, and planning for post-coaching development continuation. Each session typically lasts 60-90 minutes. Team coaching engagements usually run 6 to 9 months with monthly full-team sessions plus individual leader sessions between. Manager-as-coach programs are typically delivered as 2-3 day intensive workshops followed by 8-12 weeks of reinforcement through practice assignments, peer coaching, and coaching observation feedback. The specific structure is always customized based on organizational context, development objectives, and participant availability.

**Q5: Can coaching work in a virtual or hybrid work environment?**
Absolutely, and in many cases, virtual coaching is more effective than in-person coaching because it eliminates scheduling barriers, enables more frequent touchpoints, and allows global organizations to access coaches regardless of geographic location. Research from BetterUp and the ICF shows that virtual coaching produces outcomes equivalent to in-person coaching, with the added advantages of greater scheduling flexibility, reduced travel cost, and the ability to capture real-time coaching moments (such as preparing for a critical meeting 30 minutes beforehand). Edstellar delivers coaching across all modalities: fully virtual (video-based one-on-one sessions), hybrid (mix of in-person and virtual), and fully in-person for organizations that prefer face-to-face engagement. Our coaching technology platform supports video sessions, goal tracking, resource sharing, progress reporting, and analytics, creating a seamless coaching experience regardless of delivery format. For team coaching, we use a blend of in-person offsites for deep work sessions and virtual check-ins for ongoing accountability and progress tracking.

**Q6: How do you build a coaching culture, and how long does it take?**
Building a coaching culture is a multi-year organizational transformation, not a training program. Based on the ICF-HCI coaching culture research and Edstellar's implementation experience, most organizations progress through five maturity levels: Level 1 (Ad Hoc) where coaching is sporadic and unstructured; Level 2 (Emerging) where coaching programs exist for select populations; Level 3 (Defined) where coaching is strategically aligned with formal programs, trained internal coaches, and measurement; Level 4 (Managed) where coaching behaviors are embedded in management practice, performance systems, and organizational norms; and Level 5 (Transformative) where coaching is the primary leadership style and development mechanism across the entire organization. Moving from Level 1 to Level 3 typically takes 18-24 months with dedicated effort. Reaching Level 4-5 requires 3-5 years of sustained commitment. The process involves concurrent work on multiple fronts: training managers as coaches, developing internal coaching pools, integrating coaching into performance management, adjusting promotion criteria to reward coaching behaviors, establishing peer coaching networks, and continuously measuring and reinforcing progress. The most common mistake organizations make is treating coaching culture as a training initiative rather than a systemic change program. Edstellar's coaching culture methodology addresses all system-level levers simultaneously, which is why our clients achieve maturity level progression 40% faster than organizations attempting it independently.
